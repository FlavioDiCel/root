The copper wire had been sitting in Diego's pocket for three days , oxidized coil he'd found tucked inside his grandmother's wooden jewelry box, wrapped around a folded photograph he wasn't supposed to see. In the photograph, a young woman he almost didn't recognize stood in a Santiago street crowded with people, her fist raised, her expression fierce with something between fury and hope. On the back, in faded pencil, someone had written a single word: Resistencia.

He'd slipped both items back before Abuela Rosa noticed, but the image had followed him everywhere since-onto the bus, through the school corridors, and now into Room 14, where Señorita Isabella was uncapping a marker with the quiet authority of someone about to say something that mattered.

"Chile," Isabella said, writing the word in broad strokes across the whiteboard, "did not begin with the Spanish, and it will not end with any president." She turned to face her students - twenty-three teenagers arranged in various poses of manufactured boredom — and let the silence stretch long enough to become uncomfortable. Diego straightened almost without meaning to. There was something in her voice, a current beneath the words, like water moving fast under a calm surface.
